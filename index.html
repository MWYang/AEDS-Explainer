Maybe you've heard that New York City has been using tools like data analytics, machine learning, and AI to improve our lives.

For [fighting fires](http://www.govtech.com/public-safety/New-York-City-Fights-Fire-with-Data.html) more effectively. Or [improving high school admissions](https://www.nytimes.com/2014/12/07/nyregion/how-game-theory-helped-improve-new-york-city-high-school-application-process.html).

Or maybe you've heard that they're making things a lot worse.

Like how NYPD used computer statistics (CompStat) to target [black and Latinx communities with stop and frisks.](https://www.nyclu.org/en/stop-and-frisk-data)

Or that New York State judges set bail and sentencing using a tool called COMPAS, and the results were [biased against black people](https://www.propublica.org/article/machine-bias-risk-assessments-in-criminal-sentencing).

What these systems all have in common is that they're automated and have the potential to reproduce or worsen unjust systems.

Defining an ADS

An automated decision system (ADS) refers to any set of formal or informal processes used by a city agency or governmental body to make, or assist in making, decisions; which

  • relies on one or more components that are, partially or entirely, computerized; which
    • play a material role in the decision-making process; and where
    • the decision being made has a matieral impact on an affected individual or population.
An ADS may either be used to replicate a human decision or process, or establish a new process entirely. An ADS must have at least one computerized component that plays a material role in making or meaningfully influencing a decision.

New York Public Library uses an algorithm to stock books

A library needs books, and librarians need to pick them. But how should they do that? Libraries have limited resources and it's hard to test out new books by buying and returning them at a large scale. How can librarians tell which books will be popular with their patrons? What else should they consider when they're picking titles for their library branch?

Libraries use ESP to guess what we want to read


No, not extra-sensory perception.

ESP is an algorithm that uses library and non-library data to recommend books it thinks will be popular. Here's how the system works:
ESP is fed library patron data (like how many books by a particular author were checked out) and sales data (like Amazon purchases and reviews). In case you're wondering, no it doesn't use book reviews yet.
The ESP algorithm looks for historical patterns in the data and makes suggestions for what books to stock at which branches. For example it might predict where the young adult book "Twilight" will be popular because that neighborhood has lots of teenagers that checked out "Dracula" and "Harry Potter".
Then, it's up to the librarian to choose whether or not to follow the algorithm's advice.

So, which factors matter when ESP rates books?

We don't actually know. ESP is a [neural network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_neural_network) algorithm, which is kind of a black box. We know which datasets ESP uses, but the patterns it's using to make recommondations are fuzzy.

To be fair, we don't always know exactly what librarians are thinking either when they make decisions to stock particular books. But if they're connected with the community they serve, we might trust that and can draw from their experience and knowledge.

But we get better book picks, right?

Not so fast.

As a neural network, ESP doesn't work as well if there isn't a lot of data. For example, in established book genres and topics like knitting, it tends to pick the winners well. But it might have a harder time when guessing which debut fiction (e.g. an author’s first book) will be a big hit.

Even if we have a bunch of data from Amazon or other book sellers, the data might not be representative of many NYC communities, particularly minorities and low income individuals--some of the largest users of library services that could purchase fewer books. This means that the recommendations ESP produces might not reflect what patrons want and may not be easy to change unless we get a whole lot more data.

Also, it depends on what you mean by "better". What if your goal is to bring in people who aren't currently using the library? Existing library patron data wouldn't allow the algorithm to figure out preferences for people who don't already check out books.

Don't librarians still have final say?

Yup! Preserving librarians' decision helps to address the possible problems with ESP. Still, librarians' choices may be swayed by what seems like a concrete, numerical score based on mathematical equations.

ESP augments a librarian's choices by highlighting gaps in a librarian's knowledge and experience. On the flip side, it could sway librarians away from trusting their own instincts on what their branch needs.
