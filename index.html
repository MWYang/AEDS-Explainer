and (en)coding inequality?

Maybe you've heard that New York City has been using tools like data analytics, machine learning, and AI to improve our lives.

For fighting fires more effectively. Or improving high school admissions .

Or maybe you've heard that they're making things a lot worse.

Like how NYPD used computer statistics (CompStat) to target black and Latinx communities with stop and frisks.

Or that New York State judges set bail and sentencing using a tool called COMPAS, and the results were biased against black people .

An automated decision system (ADS) refers to any set of formal or informal processes used by a city agency or governmental body to make, or assist in making, decisions; which

  • relies on one or more components that are, partially or entirely, computerized; which
    • play a material role in the decision-making process; and where
    • the decision being made has a matieral impact on an affected individual or population.
An ADS may either be used to replicate a human decision or process, or establish a new process entirely.
An ADS must have at least one computerized component that plays a material role in making or meaningfully influencing a decision.

It sounds complicated, but we're here to walk you through [why ADSs matter](Profirio's Bodega), [how they work](Toy Algorithm), [what questions to ask](Framework), [some examples in NYC], and [what you can do about them](Get Involved).

So how does this affect New Yorkers?

Take Profirio Mejia.

He's been running P&L Deli Grocery--a quintessential New York City bodega in Washington Heights--since 2012. As a Dominican immigrant himself, Profirio knows his community well. The bodega is a place where neighbors go to watch baseball and smoke cigars, and the locals (largely low-income immigrant families) know they can depend on for groceries through the ups and downs.



Profirio works with an anti-hunger advocacy group to promote fruits and vegetables and knows the importance of the [Supplemental Nutritional Assistance Program (SNAP)] in a city where [15% of families are food insecure](https://www.foodbanknyc.org/research-reports/). More than half of his customers bought their groceries with SNAP. When their benefits hadn't come in yet, he let them pay on an IOU system, allowing long-time patrons to take home food and settle their bills later.

In 2018, a computer program almost put his bodega out of business

Porfirio got a notice from the [NYC Human Resources Administration (HRA)] that his establishment was disqualified from accepting SNAP on suspicion of food stamp fraud. Earlier that year, the city, coordinating with the [USDA], started using an computer program to find cases of trading cash for food stamps.



While the computer program hasn't been made public, it's likely that Porfirio's IOU system triggered the fraud suspicion. The [algorithm] (or set of instructions) used by the program may have noticed large one-time purchases made at P&L and mistakenly assumed it was a case of cash-for-food stamps fraud. P&L wasn't alone: the majority of businesses impacted by this new system have been small owner-owned shops in low-income neighborhoods like Porfirio's.



Porfirio is struggling to keep P&L going. His sales have dropped by 30%. The USDA told Proferio to come up with itemized receipts to prove his innocence, but Porfirio's registers were only able to print total sales figures. The letters from his patrons that he sent in as proof were deemed insufficient evidence. Porfirio has called NYC HRA, but no one has been able to reverse the decision made by the algorithm.

Using a flawed algorithm, this Automated Decision System (ADS) almost destroyed Profirio's livelihood and hurt people who really needed food.

The algorithm looked something like this

Sales data gets tracked on food-stamp cards like EBT and sent to the USDA & HRA
A computer program uses an algorithm and sales data to flag potential fraud.
if single item purchase > $100
and there are multiple such purchases
then flag store for fraud
Then, USDA & NYC HRA Staff send a fraud notice to Profirio

Why should we worry about this?

ADSs seem to keep expanding into more areas of our lives. From policing to school assignments, the decisions made by ADSs can have far-reaching consequences for all of us--sometimes good or sometimes bad.

Governments tell us they're being used in to increase efficiency and improve service-delivery (time, money, accuracy). But, as Profirio's experience shows, that might not always turn out so well.

But completely human systems also mess up, right?

Definitely. All we need to do is look back to the [history of housing segregation and redlining].

Even though human-centric systems also make mistakes, ADSs can work at a *more rapid pace and at a larger scale*. Like in Profirio's case, when they make mistakes, the ADS *might not have human checks* to override or course-correct them.

Also, implementing ADSs are often used to justify *collecting a lot more data about people*. Just like the USDA ADS flagged Profirio with reciepts of transactions, data can be used to increase policing of the marginalized communities governments are supposed to serve.

Problems like racism, discrimination, surveillance, and inequality, can get much worse, much faster with ADSs.

New York Public Library uses an algorithm to stock books

A library needs books, and librarians need to pick them. But how should they do that? Libraries have limited resources and it's hard to test out new books by buying and returning them at a large scale. How can librarians tell which books will be popular with their patrons? What else should they consider when they're picking titles for their library branch?

Libraries use ESP to guess what we want to read


No, not extra-sensory perception.

ESP is an algorithm that uses library and non-library data to recommend books it thinks will be popular. Here's how the system works:
ESP is fed library patron data (like how many books by a particular author were checked out) and sales data (like Amazon purchases and reviews). In case you're wondering, no it doesn't use book reviews yet.
The ESP algorithm looks for historical patterns in the data and makes suggestions for what books to stock at which branches. For example it might predict where the young adult book "Twilight" will be popular because that neighborhood has lots of teenagers that checked out "Dracula" and "Harry Potter".
Then, it's up to the librarian to choose whether or not to follow the algorithm's advice.

So, which factors matter when ESP rates books?

We don't actually know. ESP is a [neural network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_neural_network) algorithm, which is kind of a black box. We know which datasets ESP uses, but the patterns it's using to make recommondations are fuzzy.

To be fair, we don't always know exactly what librarians are thinking either when they make decisions to stock particular books. But if they're connected with the community they serve, we might trust that and can draw from their experience and knowledge.

But we get better book picks, right?

Not so fast.

As a neural network, ESP doesn't work as well if there isn't a lot of data. For example, in established book genres and topics like knitting, it tends to pick the winners well. But it might have a harder time when guessing which debut fiction (e.g. an author’s first book) will be a big hit.

Even if we have a bunch of data from Amazon or other book sellers, the data might not be representative of many NYC communities, particularly minorities and low income individuals--some of the largest users of library services that could purchase fewer books. This means that the recommendations ESP produces might not reflect what patrons want and may not be easy to change unless we get a whole lot more data.

Also, it depends on what you mean by "better". What if your goal is to bring in people who aren't currently using the library? Existing library patron data wouldn't allow the algorithm to figure out preferences for people who don't already check out books.

Don't librarians still have final say?

Yup! Preserving librarians' decision helps to address the possible problems with ESP. Still, librarians' choices may be swayed by what seems like a concrete, numerical score based on mathematical equations.

ESP augments a librarian's choices by highlighting gaps in a librarian's knowledge and experience. On the flip side, it could sway librarians away from trusting their own instincts on what their branch needs.
