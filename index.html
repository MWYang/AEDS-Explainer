Maybe you've heard that New York City has been using data analytics and machine learning to improve our lives.

To prevent fires by predicting and inspecting high-risk buildings.

Or rework high school admissions to more effectively match students.

Or maybe you've heard that they're making things a lot worse.

Like how NYPD used computer statistics (CompStat) to target Black and Latinx communities with stop and frisks.

Or New York State judges set bail and sentencing using a tool called COMPAS, and the results were biased against Black people.

These are all examples of an
automated decision system (ADS)
— a set of processes that rely on computerized components to make or influence a decision-making process.

ADSs sound complicated, but we'll walk through how they work and how we can prevent them from encoding inequality.

1


Why Do ADSs Matter?

Just ask Porfirio Mejia.

Since 2012, Porfirio's been running P&L Deli Grocery, a bodega in Washington Heights.*

Like many of his neighbors, Porfirio is a Dominican immigrant. The bodega is a place where neighbors go to watch baseball and smoke cigars. Locals know they can count on it for groceries, even when times are tough.

Porfirio works with an anti-hunger advocacy group and knows the importance of the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP), also known as food stamps. More than half of his customers bought their groceries with SNAP benefits from the US Department of Agriculture (USDA).

But when their benefits hadn't come in yet, he let them pay on an IOU system. That way, long-time patrons could take home food and settle their bills later.

*Original reporting by The Intercept

In 2018, a computer program almost put his bodega out of business

Porfirio got a notice from the NYC Human Resources Administration (HRA) that his establishment was disqualified from accepting SNAP on suspicion of food stamp fraud. Earlier that year, the city, coordinating with the USDA, started using a computer program to find cases of trading cash for food stamps.

While the computer program hasn't been made public, it's likely that Porfirio's IOU system triggered the fraud suspicion.

The automated decision system (ADS) looked something like this

if single item purchase more than $100
and there are multiple such purchases
then flag store for fraud
Sales data gets tracked on food-stamp cards like EBT and sent to the USDA & HRA
A computer program uses an algorithm and sales data to flag potential fraud. The algorithm used by the program may have noticed large one-time purchases made at P&L and mistakenly assumed it was a case of cash-for-food stamps fraud.
Then, USDA & NYC HRA Staff send a fraud notice to Porfirio


Porfirio is struggling to keep P&L going.

His sales have dropped by 30%.

The USDA told Porfirio to come up with itemized receipts to prove his innocence, but Porfirio's registers were only able to print total sales figures. The letters from his patrons that he sent in as proof were deemed insufficient evidence.

Porfirio has called NYC HRA, but no one has been able to reverse the decision made by the algorithm.

Using a flawed algorithm, this automated decision system almost destroyed Porfirio's livelihood and hurt people who really needed food.

Why should we worry about this?

P&L wasn't alone: the majority of businesses impacted by this new system have been small owner-owned shops in low-income neighborhoods like Porfirio's.

ADSs seem to keep expanding into more areas of our lives. From policing to school assignments, the decisions made by ADSs can have far-reaching consequences for all of us.

Governments tell us they're being used in to increase efficiency and improve service-delivery (time, money, accuracy). But, as Porfirio's experience shows, that might not always turn out so well.

But completely human systems also mess up, right?

Definitely. Just look back to the history of housing segregation and redlining.

Even though human-centric systems also make mistakes, ADSs are unique:

  • They can work at a more rapid pace and at a larger scale.
  • Like in Porfirio's case, when they make mistakes, the ADS might not have human checks to override or course-correct them.
  • Also, implementing ADSs are often used to justify collecting a lot more data about people.
  • Just like the USDA ADS flagged Porfirio with reciepts of transactions, data can be used to increase policing of the marginalized communities governments are supposed to serve.

Problems like racism, discrimination, surveillance, and inequality, can get much worse, much faster with ADSs.

What are Algorithms?

They're like instructions.

Like in Porfirio's case, all ADSs use computer programs that are made up of algorithms (detailed sets of automated, computerized instructions). These algorithms work together to complete a task.

Just think about how you buy an avocado.

What factors do you consider?

I want one that's affordable.

Hopefully it's organic, but less than $1.50.

I'm going to make some guacamole to eat tonight,
so I want the ripest one I can find.

Hover over to find the best avocado.

All of these calculations you're doing in your head are an algorithm.

You’ve taken some variables (the price, organic or not, how ripe it is, etc.) to analyze a set of data points (the pile of avocados) and reach a decision (which avocado should you buy).

What kind of algorithms do ADSs use?

It depends. They can sometimes be a bit more complicated than the ones we use in our everyday decisions.

Some ADS involve multiple algorithms, hundreds of thousands of data points, and variables that are given different weights. But really, the building blocks are the same.

In some cases, people have control over what steps the algorithm takes. For example, people — like you — might decide which variables are the most important in buying avocados or predicting a place that is committing food-stamp fraud.

In other cases, data is given to a computer, and that computer analyses the data, learns about it, and then decides which variables are important.

When a computer learns on its own rather than you telling it which variables are important, this is called a
machine learning algorithm. They are less transparent because we don't always know why the computer places more or less importance of different variables.

ADSs can involve multiple algorithms, hundreds of thousands of data points, and variables that are given different weights chosen by the computer. But ultimately, the building blocks are the same.

Frameworks

So are ADSs good or bad?

Unfortunately, there isn't a simple answer. But in order to start tackling this question we need to develop a framework to think critically about ADSs.

Remember, ADSs are systems that involve both algorithms and humans. Implementing an ADS is an opportunity to undo unjust systems.

Any ADS that does not undo injustice in the system is simply replicating a status quo in which injustices exist. Because ADSs work faster than humans, they have the potential to reproduce or worsen unjust systems.

What are the components of an ADS that we can evaluate to check for this?

A good ADS starts with a good purpose

How does the agency define the goal of the ADS and why are they automating a component of the decision making process?

Purpose will frame our thinking. It is the first step before we do our evaluation.

Some purposes cause more reason for concern than others.

  • Good Purpose

    A purpose like "to improve the lives of New Yorkers and undo unjust systems" tends to be really good and not cause much concern because it means the ADS could be designed with that potential of unjust systems in mind.

  • Neutral Purpose

    A purpose like "to do an existing task more efficiently" is a common purpose governments use for why they are automating part of a decision making process. This purpose can be okay, but it doesn't explicitly acknowledge unjust systems, meaning it might accidentally reproduce, worsen, or accelarate an unjust system. We want to be critical of these sorts of purposes.

  • Bad Purpose

    A purpose like "to cut services or benefits from vulnerable people" is very problematic. If this is the purpose, we might want to totally reject this project because it will almost definitely reproduce, worsen, or accelerate an unjust system.

If we like the purpose enough we should start thinking about the technical components.

We should make sure they match. Otherwise, the ADS might accidentally reproduce, worsen, or accelerate unjust systems.

Let's look at Porfirio's story through this framework.

Impact

Who does the ADS affect and how?

Impact helps us think through how important the discrimination / discriminatory outcomes (if they exist) are.

  • Scale
    How many people might be affected by the ADS.
  • Scope

    How deep of an impact the decision of the ADS might have on people. Deep impact is when the decision impacts something important, knowing which building might catch on fire is a matter of life and death.

    Superficial impact is when the decision impacts something not important or the impact is not very severe, like knowing which books will be the most popular is a matter of buying and shelving books.

  • Vulnerability

    How vulnerable are the groups that the ADS impacts. If an ADS impacts a group that has historically faced discrimination and the the ADS takes away resources from that group, the ADS is probably going to reproduce or worsening inequality.

Porfirio's Story:
Impact

Purpose: To Use government funds more efficiently by catching food stamp fraud

Impact

Scale

Large. The USDA algorithm affects any business that accepts food stamps. In New York City, that's x% of all grocery and convenient stores.

Scope

Deep. The loss of revenue for Porfirio was signficant. Moreover, the community lost a reliable food source.

Vulnerability

In general the USDA algorithm impacts vulnerable communities because it impacts communites that rely on alternative financial systems, who are usually lower income and communities of color.

Bias

What inputs in the ADS produce problems?

It helps identify what parts of the algorithm used in an ADS have bias imbedded in them and thus create discriminatory outcomes or worsen unjust systems.

  • Data

    Data are never objective. We embed our human bias when we collect, store, and use data. All data are biased.

    Data being biased means that when we analyze the data, the data might tell us to do something that's not purely objective. It might tell us to do something based on the existing biases of the world that it's capturing.

    For example, if we use arrest data by neighborhood to distribute the police force, then we may send police to the same neighborhoods that they have historically policed, which leads to more collection of arrest data in those same neighborhoods. This creates a vicious cycle.

    These biases can arise because all datasets, to some extent, are incomplete, don't accurately represent the thing they're supposed to represent, and have historical mistakes that new data is building on.

  • Algorithm Model

    An ADS should make sure it's using an algorithm that is appropriate for the question it's answering and handling the variables correctly. Chosing the right algorithm should relate to the ADS's purpose.

    If humans are determining specific components of the algorithm, this can also create bias. For many algorithms, humans will be determining the outcome of interest and the variables.

    These decisions are biased because they are based on our own world views and what we think are the relationships between outcomes of intrests and variables.

Porfirio's Story:
Bias

Purpose: To Use government funds more efficiently by catching food stamp fraud

Bias

Data

Because Porfirio's bodega is in a low-income neighborhood, he is more likely to have more customers with food stamps. This is a bias in the data about bodegas in poor neighborhoods. This means that bodegas like Porfirio's receive more scrunity whereas a bodega in a rich neighborhood gets less scrunity.

Algorithm Model

The algorithm the USDA used to identify cash-for-food-stamps fraud was using large one time purchases as variable to indicate fraud. The algorithm couldn't distinguish between true fraud and Porfirio's IOU system. Someone decided that the model should flag certain transactions as fraudulent, so there was bias in the model against those kinds of transactions.

Explainability

How easy is it for humans to understand how the ADS works?

When we know how the ADS works, we can analyze its process and components. If the ADS is has discriminatory outcomes or is worsening unjust systems, then we can pinpoint exactly how it's happening.

  • Easy to Understand

    Some algorithms are very easy to understand, like instructions for choosing an avocado. We know exactly which variables are going into the model and how they're weighted.

    The avocado algorithm is a decision tree. Super explainable.

  • Hard to Understand

    Others can feel like a black box. We give the data to the computer and it gives us a decision, but we don't really know how it reached that decision.

    A neural network algorithm is not that explainable.

Porfirio's Story:
Explainability

Purpose: To Use government funds more efficiently by catching food stamp fraud

Explainability

For Porfirio, we don't know how the whole ADS works. Who looks at the results of the algorithm that is flagging cases of supposed fraud? What are the checks and balances around the decision making process?

If we knew more about the process and components of the ADS, we would be able to see if there were design decisions to mitigate mistakes like miscategorizing Porfirio's bodega.

Automation

How much of the decision is made by the ADS?

If an ADS relies heavily on the outputs of the algorithmic component, it could generate discriminatory outcomes or worsen unjust systems unchecked by humans.

  • Low Automation

    The ADS doesn't make a decision. It might simply analyses the data which is then used to inform a human decision.

  • Medium Automation

    The algorithm provides an advisory decision, but a human still looks over the decision and decides whether to implement it.

  • High Automation

    An algorithm makes a decision and the people using it simply implement that decision.

Automation

High

As far we know, the USDA algorithm automatically flags transactions it categorizes as "fraud" which results in the USDA sending out a letter to a business owner notifying them that their ability to accept SNAP will be suspended unless they prove otherwise.

It is unclear whether a USDA employee reviews the flagged transactions before such a letter is sent.

Flexibility

Can the ADS be changed easily if people have feedback?

In order to avoid creating discriminatory outcomes or worsening unjust systems, we want to make sure if there's a problem with the ADS that we know to fix it.

  • Feedback Mechanisms
    Feedback from people directly affected by the ADS or who use the ADS on a regular basis is often the best source of feedback to improve the ADS.
  • Ability and Access to Update

    While feedback is important, what a government can do with the feedback is limited based on whether or not they have access and skills to update the algorithm.

    Whether or not they have access to update the algorithm might depend on who owns ADS. Is it owned by the government or by a private company? And it might be based on whether the government has the in house talent to update the algorithm.

Flexibility

Feedback System

The USDA ADS as a whole is not very flexible because there is not a good feedback system from people who are directly affected by the ADS.

Although business owners have the ability to submit feedback if they believe they've been wrongly flagged, this feedback has to be in a very specific form. Moreover, there is no mechanism for businesses or others to tell the USDA how it can improve its system.

Ability and Access to Update

We're not sure!

Now you know how to ask the right questions.

The purpose and evaluation criteria for the technical components of an ADS are just a start to help you think critically about ADSs.

These things all interact with one another in imperfect ways. For example, we hope that government thinks about their purposes when making decisions about technical components, but sometimes they forget the purpose. As another example, some of the technical components relate to one another, like the more explainable an ADS is (explainability) the easier it is it give feedback and be able to update it (flexibility).

We hope that this provides a baseline understanding about how to think about ADSs and how to advocate for good ADSs.

Examples

Learn about some real ADSs being used in NYC.

New York Public Library uses an algorithm to stock books

A library needs books, and librarians need to pick them. But how should they do that? Libraries have limited resources and it's hard to test out new books by buying and returning them at a large scale. How can librarians tell which books will be popular with their patrons? What else should they consider when they're picking titles for their library branch?

Libraries use ESP to guess what we want to read


No, not extra-sensory perception.

ESP is an algorithm that uses library and non-library data to recommend books it thinks will be popular. Here's how the system works:
The library feeds ESP is fed circulation data (like which books have been checked out) and sales data (like Amazon purchases). In case you're wondering, no it doesn't use book reviews yet.
The ESP algorithm looks for historical patterns in the data and makes suggestions for what books to stock at which branches. For example it might predict where the young adult book "Twilight" will be popular because that neighborhood has lots of teenagers that checked out "Dracula" and "Harry Potter".
Then, it's up to the librarian to choose whether or not to follow the algorithm's advice.

So, which factors matter when ESP rates books?

We don't actually know. ESP is a neural network algorithm, which is kind of a black box. We know which datasets ESP uses, but the patterns it's using to make recommendations are fuzzy.

To be fair, we don't always know exactly what librarians are thinking either when they make decisions to stock particular books. But if they're connected with the community they serve, we might trust that and can draw from their experience and knowledge.

But we get better book picks, right?

Not so fast.

As a neural network, ESP doesn't work as well if there isn't a lot of data. For example, in established book genres and topics like knitting, it tends to pick the winners well. But it might have a harder time when guessing which debut fiction (e.g. an author’s first book) will be a hit with readers

Even if we have a bunch of data from Amazon or other book sellers, the data might not be representative of many NYC communities, particularly minorities and low income individuals--some of the largest users of library services that could purchase fewer books. This means that the recommendations ESP produces might not reflect what patrons want and may not be easy to change unless we get a whole lot more data.

Also, it depends on what you mean by "better". What if your goal is to bring in people who aren't currently using the library? Existing library patron data wouldn't allow the algorithm to figure out preferences for people who don't already check out books.

Don't librarians still have final say?

Yup! Preserving librarians' power to make decisions helps to address the possible problems with ESP. Still, librarians' choices may be swayed by what seems like a concrete, numerical score based on mathematical equations.

ESP augments a librarian's choices by highlighting gaps in a librarian's knowledge and experience. On the flip side, it could sway librarians away from trusting their own instincts on what their branch needs.
